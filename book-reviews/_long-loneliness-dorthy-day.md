# Overview
For those of you not familiar with Dorothy Day, she was a journalist, a pacifist and an anarchist who lived from 1897 to 1980. She is most known for her founding role in the Catholic Worker Movement and corresponding newspaper, the Catholic Worker. The Catholic Worker Movement is known for houses of hospitality where the poor are welcomed. The Long Loneliness is Dorothy's autobiography. Check out her [wikipedia page](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorothy_Day) to learn more.

# Are people essentially good or essentially bad? (257)
Each person has a perspective on what it means to be human. I call this a working anthropology. Often, our working anthropologies are osmosed from our culture and I was no different. From the old adage, "Well, you're only human," to the explicit Calvinist belief in total depravity, to one of my favorite songs, Human, by Jon Belion, where the chorus rings out, "I'm just so sick of being human," I found myself puzzling about life on earth with an ingrained belief that my humanity was essentially bad, a barrier to achieving my true potential.

Dorothy recounts a parallel struggle with her own understanding of what it meant to be human. The apparent division between the body and soul was troubling. In her adolescence this binary seemed strong, with the body and its desires being essentially evil, and the things of the soul essentially good. She longed to escape her body and the evil that came with it.

Toward the end of her autobiography she shares memories of a retreat where a priest, Father Hugo, weaved together the disparate threads of body and soul teaching that, "the body is no less good than the soul," and that, "We are not to destroy but to transform it (the body)." Dorothy recounts notes she took while listening to Father Hugo; "Although human nature is essentially good it is infected by original sin, which is forgiven at baptism, but the effects will remain in the soul. We are prone to evil. We have a bias toward evil." 

Dorothy’s retelling of her struggle to bring together the physical and the spiritual was a catalyst for reflection on my working anthropology and the nuanced and dynamic relationship between the good and evil in each of us. What do you think? Are humans essentially good, or essentially evil? Why?

# Anarchists are optimists (269)
Dorothy Day and the Catholic Worker Movement advocated for a form of political anarchy that her coworker, Bob Ludlow, defined as the, “advocacy of decentralized self-governing bodies,” where, “the whole people composing a community should take care of what governing is to be done rather than have a distant and centralized State do it.” According to him, “Anarchists advocate directive government (mutual aid) but reject coercive government (the State).”

Any form of self government requires profound confidence that individuals will engage and work for the common good. It requires optimism to advocate for a political system where the most pressing and complex issues are in the hands of the masses. This applies to less direct forms of self government, such as in the United States; it is especially true of anarchy. Anarchy places a high level of responsibility on the people if they are to, “take care of what governing is to be done.”

Bob Ludlow goes on. “Our Lord taught us to pray ‘Thy kingdom come on earth as it is in heaven’ — in other words, the nearer earthly government approximates what things are done in heaven the more Christian it is. I do believe— whether it can be realized or not— that the anarchist society approaches nearer to this ideal than do other forms of government. As the Christian lives in hope so may we set this as the idea, towards which we work even if it seems as impractical as Calvary.”

Dorothy does not provide a systematic breakdown of how Ludlow arrives at the conclusion that anarchy comes closest to, “Thy kingdom come,” but it seems to be rooted in a working anthropology that takes seriously the implications of humans being made in the image of God. The image of God, understood as a vocation to love God and people and to work and keep the earth is a great responsibility. It brings with it the potential for great good, and with the fall also carries the likelihood of great evil.

As I shared earlier, my default working anthropology skews low; I tend to be skeptical that people will be able to promote the good. For example, this meme was something I strongly resonated with in high school, and if I am honest, it still resonates to some degree.

# Do great lights blind people? (265)
Dorothy shares a memory of Ammon Hennacy, a writer for the Catholic Worker. Ammon who, stuck in solitary confinement during World War 1, read the Bible, Tolstoy and Gandhi. These influences lead him to advocate for a form of Christian anarchy. Upon reading one of Ammon’s articles, the rector of a seminary remarked that Ammon, “has seen so great a light it has blinded him.”

I found this idea, that someone could encounter ideas so powerful it blinds them to reality, compelling. But it also reminded me of a contrasting account of those who have encountered a great light: Plato’s Cave. In Plato’s Cave allegory he describes a group of prisoners who have lived their whole lives chained to a cave, facing a blank wall. The prisoners see shadows projected against the blank wall as objects pass behind a fire located behind them. These flickering shadows are the prisoners’ reality. However, one prisoner escapes the cave and experiences the great light of day. He realizes that the shadows are a piss poor representation of true reality, and he resolves to free the other prisoners so they too can experience the great light of day. However, when he returns to the cave talking about how the shadows are not true to reality, just poor projections of the real thing, the prisoners think he is crazy, that he, “has seen so great a light it has blinded him.”

People on the fringe of society are often treated like the prisoner who returns to enlighten the other inmates. Dorothy Day was certainly on the fringe of society and held extreme ideas to be true (e.g. pacifism and anarchism). Do you think she encountered true reality and was honestly living it out? Did she see a light so great it blinded her? Or is she just crazy? When you encounter someone on the fringe of society, how seriously do you take their perspective?
